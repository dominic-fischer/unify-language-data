  Notes:
    - "Personal pronouns inflect for person, number, and case; third-person forms also inflect for gender."
    - "Literary third-person subject pronouns distinguish animate (egli, ella) vs inanimate (esso, essa) antecedents; colloquial usage prefers lui, lei, loro for animate subjects, and has no specific inanimate subject pronoun (demonstratives such as questo/quello may be used if needed)."
    - "The pronoun 'ciò' is uninflected and used only with abstract antecedents."
    - "The forms ci, vi, ne can function both as personal pronouns (instrumental/genitive) and as clitic pro-forms for 'there' (ci/vi) and 'from there' (ne)."
    - "Clitic pronouns generally precede the verb, but can be enclitic in certain constructions; in infinitive, gerund, and (except with third-person courtesy forms) imperative moods, clitics must be attached as enclitics."
    - "Stressed forms of non-subject cases are used for emphasis."
    - "In colloquial speech, redundant combinations such as 'a me mi...' are widely used but considered non-standard."
    - "More than one clitic can attach to a single verb; typically two, occasionally three for some speakers; accusative+genitive is excluded from two-clitic combinations; order follows stated patterns."
    - "Clitic forms (except cui) may be apocopated before vowel-initial verbal forms; apocopation is optional."
    - "Italian has a T–V distinction: informal 2nd-person is 'tu'; formal address uses 3rd-person 'Lei' (sg.) and historically 'Loro' (pl.); these take 3rd-person verb conjugations; formal plural is rarely used in modern Italian."
